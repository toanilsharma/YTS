DP LEVEL
The Workhorse Sensor
It's the most common, but also the most misunderstood... and miscalibrated.
Pressure is NOT Level.
prepared by https://designcalculators.co.in
The Principle
(Hydrostatic Head)
P = h × g × ρ
Pressure = Height × Gravity × Density
A DP sensor *infers* Height (Level) by measuring Pressure.
This fails if Density (ρ) changes!
prepared by https://designcalculators.co.in
The Magic Number
Specific Gravity (SG)
SG = Density Fluid / Density Water
Water SG = 1.0
Oil SG ≈ 0.85
Caustic SG ≈ 1.5
All calcs MUST use SG! (e.g., in "Inches of Water Column")

Case 1: Open Tank
(Vented to Atmosphere)
Simple & Common
HP Tap: Bottom of tank (0% level).
LP Tap: Open to atmosphere.
(e.g., Water Sump, Storage Tank)
prepared by https://designcalculators.co.in
Open Tank: The CALC
(Range: 0 - 100 inches)
Fluid: Oil (SG 0.85)
LRV (4mA): 0" Level
0" × 0.85 SG = 0 inH₂O
URV (20mA): 100" Level
100" × 0.85 SG = 85 inH₂O

Case 2: Closed Tank
(DRY LEG)
For Non-Condensing Gas
(e.g., Air, N₂ Blanket)
HP Tap: Bottom (0% level).
LP Tap: Top (Vapor Space).
The LP leg stays empty ("Dry").
prepared by https://designcalculators.co.in
Dry Leg: The CALC
(Range: 0 - 100 inches)
Fluid: Oil (SG 0.85)
LRV (4mA): 0" Level
DP = P High - P Low
DP = (P Gas) - (P Gas) = 0 inH₂O
URV (20mA): 100" Level
DP = (100" × 0.85 + P Gas) - (P Gas)
DP = 85 inH₂O
(Same as Open Tank!)
prepared by https://designcalculators.co.in
Case 3: Closed Tank
(WET LEG)
For Condensing Vapor
(e.g., Steam Drum, Solvents)
LP Tap: Top (Vapor Space).
The LP leg is *filled* with a liquid (e.g., water, SG 1.0).
This creates a SUPPRESSED range.
prepared by https://designcalculators.co.in
Wet Leg: The CALC
(Range: 0-100", Leg=120", SG=1.0)
LRV (4mA): 0" Level
DP = P High - P Low
DP = (P Gas) - (120" × 1.0 + P Gas)
DP = -120 inH₂O
URV (20mA): 100" Level (SG 0.85)
DP = (100" × 0.85 + P Gas) - (120" × 1.0 + P Gas)
DP = 85 - 120 = -35 inH₂O

The Manifold
(3-Valve or 5-Valve)
Your Best Friend
Allows isolation and equalization for safe zeroing and calibration.
Block Valves (2): Isolate HP & LP from process.
Equalizing Valve (1): Puts HP = LP.
Vent/Cal Valves (2): (On 5-valve) For bleeding and applying test pressure.
prepared by https://designcalculators.co.in
Manifold: Zero Check
(Under Pressure)
1. CLOSE the LP Block Valve.
2. OPEN the Equalizing Valve.
3. (Wait) HP & LP are now equal.
4. Sensor output should be 0 DP.
5. Adjust sensor Zero if needed.
6. CLOSE Equalizing Valve.
7. OPEN LP Block Valve (slowly!).
(DO NOT TOUCH HP BLOCK)

The #1 Error Source
DENSITY (SG) CHANGE
Temperature is the Enemy!
Your calibration (e.g., 85 inH₂O) is ONLY valid at ONE temperature.
If fluid gets hotter, its SG *drops*.
The transmitter will read LOW, even if the level is correct!
prepared by https://designcalculators.co.in
The Fix: Density Comp
(e.g., Boiler Drums)
This is a DCS/PLC Job
1. DP Sensor measures `h * ρ`.
2. A *second* transmitter measures Pressure (for steam) or Temp.
3. The DCS calculates the *real* density (ρ) from a steam table.
4. `Level = (h * ρ) / ρ`

Expert App: INTERFACE
(Oil SG 0.8 / Water SG 1.0)
Range: 0-50" Interface
LRV (4mA): 0" Water (50" Oil)
DP = 50" × 0.8 = 40 inH₂O
URV (20mA): 50" Water (0" Oil)
DP = 50" × 1.0 = 50 inH₂O
Span = 50 - 40 = 10 inH₂O
(Span = H × (SG Heavy - SG Light))

CRITICAL: Dos & Don'ts
DO: Slope impulse lines (drain liquids from gas, vent gas from liquids).
DO: Calibrate for the *correct* SG at operating temperature.
DO: Fill a Wet Leg *before* zeroing.
DON'T: Apply a "Square Root" function. That is for FLOW.
DON'T: Confuse Level with Volume (unless the tank is linear).
DON'T: Leave the Equalizing valve open!
